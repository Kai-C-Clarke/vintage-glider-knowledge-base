# Electrical Bonding in Vintage Wooden Gliders — Why It's Absent
## Compiled June 2026
## Source: Reasoned analysis by Google Gemini AI, June 2026
## Attribution: This note represents reasoned engineering analysis, not a primary regulatory source.
## It should be read alongside AC43.13-1B Chapter 11 (Grounding and Bonding) in this Archive.

---

## Summary

Vintage wooden gliders such as the Slingsby Prefect, Schleicher Ka-8, and similar types
do not have the bonding networks, grounding straps, copper ribbons, or static wicks seen
on modern sailplanes. This is not an oversight — it reflects the materials, electronics,
and operational context of early aviation.

---

## 1. Wood Naturally Dissipates Static Charge

Bone-dry wood is an insulator, but the spruce, plywood, and fabric coverings of vintage
gliders absorb ambient atmospheric humidity. This microscopic moisture level makes the
wood slightly conductive — acting as a high-resistance dielectric. Static charges built
up from air friction leak slowly and naturally through the wooden frame to the metal
control linkages, dissipating safely without creating a single massive arc.

This is fundamentally different from modern GRP and carbon fibre structures, which are
completely sealed and highly resistive, requiring deliberate bonding networks to provide
a safe discharge path.

---

## 2. No Electronics — Nothing to Protect

The primary reason modern aircraft use extensive bonding is to prevent precipitation
static (p-static) from interfering with onboard radios and avionics.

Classic wooden gliders were designed to fly on purely mechanical instruments — mechanical
variometers, altimeters, turn-and-slip indicators. No electrical systems, no batteries,
no radios. With nothing sensitive onboard to protect from electromagnetic interference
(EMI), there was no engineering need for a bonding network.

---

## 3. VFR Operation — Avoiding High-Charge Environments

Vintage gliders were built for Visual Flight Rules (VFR) operation in clear weather.
They were not intended to fly inside cumulus clouds or active convective systems where
severe static buildup and lightning occur. Because they were kept clear of clouds,
early designers had no requirement to incorporate lightning protection paths.

Note: If a wooden glider is struck by lightning, the high resistance of the wood causes
the moisture inside to instantly boil and vaporise, which can shatter the structure.
This is a known hazard — another reason vintage wooden gliders should avoid cloud.

---

## 4. Isolated Metal Components

In a vintage wooden glider, metal components are limited to control rods, cables, and
hinges — physically separated by large expanses of wood and fabric. These isolated metal
parts cannot easily act together to accumulate the dangerous electrical potentials that
build up across a large single-piece carbon fibre wing.

---

## 5. Retrofitting Modern Electronics

If a vintage wooden glider is fitted with a modern VHF radio or digital transponder,
localised bonding becomes necessary. Mechanics will typically need to run bonding wires
to control sticks and instrument panels to prevent the radio crackling through dry air
or light precipitation.

Any such installation should be documented on BGA Form 205 as a modification and
assessed under AMP 2-3 (Modification of non-Part 21 gliders) if not covered by the
manufacturer's original documentation.

For bonding methods, refer to AC43.13-1B Chapter 11 Section 15 (Grounding and Bonding)
in this Archive.
