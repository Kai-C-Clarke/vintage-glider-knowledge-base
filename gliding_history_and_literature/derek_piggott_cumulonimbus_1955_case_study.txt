# Derek Piggott — Cumulonimbus Encounter, Lasham, 14 July 1955
## A Case Study in Static Discharge and Lightning Strike on a Wooden Glider
## Compiled June 2026
## Sources: Royal Meteorological Society report (Piggott, 1955); SSA obituary; press accounts
## Analysis: Google Gemini AI, June 2026, reviewed by Jon Stiles BGA Inspector I/C1408

---

## Overview

On 14 July 1955, Derek Piggott — gliding instructor, military pilot, and future author of
numerous gliding textbooks — flew a Slingsby Skylark 2 directly into a massive cumulonimbus
cloud at Lasham Airfield, Hampshire, in an attempt on the British altitude record.

He reached approximately 22,800 feet. He was struck by lightning four or five times.
He received electric shocks he described as feeling like "holding on to the 230-volt mains
and being unable to let go." He had no oxygen. He survived, landed safely, and lived to
the age of 96.

His own account, published in the Royal Meteorological Society journal (1955), remains
one of the most vivid primary source descriptions of what happens to a wooden glider —
and its pilot — inside a severe thunderstorm.

---

## The Aircraft

**Type:** Slingsby Skylark 2
**Structure:** Wood and fabric — spruce primary structure, plywood skinning, fabric covering
**Electrical systems:** None
**Lightning protection:** None
**Bonding network:** None

The Skylark 2 had internal metal control cables, rods, and fittings — physically isolated
from each other by large expanses of wood and fabric, with no intentional electrical
continuity between them. This is the standard configuration of vintage wooden gliders
of the period (see Archive note: Electrical Bonding in Vintage Wooden Gliders).

---

## What Happened — The Electrical Sequence

As Piggott was violently carried upward inside the storm, static charge accumulated on
the aircraft's metal control components. With no bonding network to provide a continuous
discharge path, potential built up until it arced:

**Electric shocks through the control column:**
Piggott reported receiving shocks through the control stick that grew progressively more
violent, eventually feeling "like holding on to the 230-volt mains and being unable to let go."
This is the classic symptom of an unbonded metal control circuit accumulating triboelectric
charge with no path to dissipate it.

**Electrical arc in the cockpit:**
A blue electrical arc approximately one inch wide shot across the inside of the nose cone
near his rudder pedals, shocking his feet.

**Direct lightning strikes:**
The glider was struck directly by lightning four or five times around the cockpit walls.
The wooden structure absorbed and survived these strikes — the low conductivity of the
wood prevented the instantaneous explosive vaporisation that would occur in a drier
or more resistive structure, and there was no fuel to ignite.

---

## Compounding Factors

**Ice on control surfaces:**
The moisture in the storm froze onto the wooden wing profiles. Ice accumulated to the
point where control surfaces jammed mechanically. Piggott had to physically break the
ice by muscling the controls to maintain directional control in severe turbulence.

**Hypoxia:**
At approximately 23,000 feet without supplemental oxygen, Piggott was operating in
conditions that cause severe hypoxia — confusion, euphoria, impaired judgement.
He later stated that the electric shocks became too violent to bear before hypoxia
would have rendered him incapable of flying the aircraft.

---

## Why He Survived

1. **Wood absorbed the lightning.** The moisture content of the wooden structure
   provided a high-resistance path that dissipated the strike energy without
   explosive vaporisation of the airframe.

2. **No fuel.** No ignition source, no fuel — lightning cannot cause fire in a
   pure wooden glider.

3. **Exceptional skill.** Piggott was a highly experienced military pilot and instructor.
   He maintained aircraft control through violent turbulence, ice-jammed controls,
   hypoxia, and repeated electric shocks simultaneously.

4. **Luck.** The lightning struck around rather than directly through the cockpit.

---

## Lessons for the Archive

This incident illustrates in the most vivid terms the mechanisms described in the
Archive note on electrical bonding in vintage wooden gliders:

- Unbonded metal control circuits will accumulate static charge in high-static environments
- The charge will arc when potential is sufficient — through the pilot if the pilot is
  in the circuit
- Wood's natural moisture content provides passive (unintentional) static dissipation
  under normal conditions, but is overwhelmed inside a cumulonimbus
- Vintage wooden gliders have no lightning protection and should never be flown near
  active convective cloud

If a vintage glider is retrofitted with radio or transponder equipment, the installation
of localised bonding becomes important — see Archive note on electrical bonding and
AC43.13-1B Chapter 11 Section 15.
