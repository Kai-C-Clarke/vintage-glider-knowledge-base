FOREWORD

If one travels through Germany today, it not infrequently happens that one comes unexpectedly upon a nondescript shed in some out-of-the-way village, where busy hands are assembling the framework of an aircraft. Drawing closer, one learns that the village youth have built this aircraft themselves in the shed, which on closer inspection reveals itself as a simple but serviceable workshop, and are now presenting it for inspection to the building examiner called in from the nearest town.

Aircraft construction in the village, carried out with more enthusiasm than technical knowledge, yet with such care and precision that the flight-eager village youth can entrust themselves with confidence to their self-built aircraft, even in flight!

This small soaring episode, which one can witness today in any Rhön village or in Württemberg, in Hesse, Silesia, East Prussia, and Westphalia, shows how thoroughly the sailplane sport has taken root among us, and how it not only makes a sporting flying spirit into a universal matter of course, but also, through the self-construction of aircraft, awakens previously unknown technical abilities in the youth of our people, regardless of place of residence, social standing, or education.

Since soaring groups have abandoned their go-it-alone approach to design and construction, and have recognized that the adoption of suitable, proven aircraft designs — for which they can readily obtain all necessary documentation — is the most advantageous path, this widespread practice of self-building gliders and sailplanes has been able to establish itself throughout Germany.

Despite all plans and instructions, self-construction initially costs dearly in lessons learned. What an abundance of craft-related ex-

FOREWORD

New aviation clubs are being founded throughout all parts of Germany on a continuing basis. All of them wish to build their training and soaring aircraft in their own workshops. In doing so, each one must again gather the experiences that can only be acquired through years of practice. Setbacks due to faulty construction occur easily. The enthusiasm for self-building is gone when the building inspector has examined the aircraft and identified the many deficiencies, some of which can be remedied only with great difficulty or only at the cost of considerable additional work.

How many mistakes would have been avoided if an experienced workshop supervisor had provided proper guidance. No one involved in building gliders in the workshop will be spared from paying their tuition, so to speak. This book has a twofold purpose. On the one hand it is intended to identify the difficulties that, as experience shows, arise in every build; and on the other hand, by means of explanatory illustrations, to provide the means of avoiding them. This book is intended to reduce the amount of that tuition. It is therefore addressed primarily to the young and still inexperienced glider builder, but the seasoned and experienced practitioner will also find here and there a useful pointer regarding something he has not yet tried himself.

THE WORKSHOP AND ITS EQUIPMENT.

The room in which we wish to set up our workshop must meet a number of requirements. Depending on the building programme we intend to carry out in our workshop, it must be of sufficient size. The length of the fuselage or wing determines the minimum length of the workshop in one direction. We must also bear in mind that in addition to final assembly, individual components must be manufactured as well. Are the doors or other access openings large enough to remove the finished parts? It is a bitter lesson when this question is considered too late and doors or window openings have to be enlarged.

In winter the workshop must be heatable, as fresh glue joints are rendered unusable by frost.

It is practical if our workshop is situated near a larger open area where our aircraft can be assembled.

Following the proverb "Mastery reveals itself within limitations," we can manage with very minimal workshop equipment, particularly when building beginner aircraft. Only gradually, as building activity grows, should the equipment be expanded!

THE MATERIALS.

The materials from which aircraft are made must be selected with particular care, since maximum strength combined with the lowest possible weight is required of them. In the design of gliders, the material dimensions (especially for wooden and metal fitting components) are always calculated on the basis of good-quality material.

Our principal building material is wood, and to this we shall devote our greatest attention. Since we mostly select the wood ourselves, we must also be in a position to judge its suitability for our purposes. For the other materials mentioned later, such as sheet steel, cable, bolts, etc., procurement is considerably simpler, since these are mostly standardised and their quality can be expressed numerically when ordering. WOOD.

The most frequently used timbers are pine, spruce, and ash. Polish pine, or the equivalent East Prussian pine, is particularly suitable due to its well-aligned grain. Occasionally pine is also replaced by American Oregon pine, which is distinguished by a very fine grain. However, with the latter type, care must be taken when selecting, as the grain may run true for several metres and then suddenly deviate sharply from the straight.

The selection of wood for specific structural members falls into two main parts:

1. Selecting at the timber yard, and
2. cutting the strips from the planks.

What requirements must we now place on our planks at the timber yard? What must they look like? From the construction drawings we have established clearly which timber species we need. The cross-sections of the strips to be cut from the plank also determine the required thickness of the material.

At the timber yard the trees are stacked cut into individual boards; from the individual logs we must always select the right planks suited to our purposes. Figure 5 shows us a log in cross-section,

## Wood Selection

The trunk is separated into individual boards stacked in layers. Approximately one third of the trunk diameter is occupied by the heartwood, which can be identified by its darker coloration. At the very centre of the heartwood lies the pith. This must not be used for structural components. The heartwood itself is particularly well suited for compression struts (stanchions). Surrounding the heartwood is the sapwood, which, when cut from the region nearest the heartwood, is suitable for spar flanges and similar components. From the illustration we can see the approximate position of the planks that yield the most useful material for our purposes. Precise specifications cannot naturally be given, since every trunk presents a different appearance. The essential point, however, is that we require the sapwood planks lying approximately halfway between the heartwood and the bark. The timber merchant will not be particularly pleased when we select the best planks from the middle of his trunk; no matter — insist on it. For our aircraft we need the very best available.

---

## Grain Inspection

We must now examine the grain of the plank. It should show long, straight, and fine grain — completely free of knots, without wind shakes, and with as few resin pockets as possible. From Figure 6 we can clearly identify the two pieces with poor grain and the one with good grain. This gives us a general picture of what our timber should not look like. Furthermore, ensure that our planks are not musty — that is, showing staining — and that they exhibit neither blue stain nor red rot. Finally, the trunk may exhibit spiral grain, which can be recognised by the helical pattern of the bark. Spar flanges, particularly box spar flanges, cut from such a trunk will warp.

---

## Moisture Content

The timber must have been stored in the open air (not in a drying chamber), but kept under cover. The moisture content must lie between 14 and 16%. We determine the moisture content of a plank by weighing a test piece, drying it completely in an oven, and determining the percentage loss in weight; this must then lie between 14 and 16%.

---

## Cutting the Planks

When the plank is subsequently cut into strips, the two outermost sections are discarded (being the softer part of the sapwood adjacent to the bast). Cut into individual strips as closely as possible along the direction of the grain.

WHICH TYPES OF WOOD DO WE USE FOR SPECIFIC
STRUCTURAL MEMBERS?

For all highly stressed parts such as spars, struts, and fuselage frames heavily loaded by the wings, we most advantageously use pine, or alternatively well-grained spruce.

For ribs, trailing edges, and other parts that serve as covering, one preferably uses lighter wood such as spruce, lime, or again fir.

In many cases it is not entirely clear in the workshop which wood may be used. One must always strive to build as light as possible. Parts that serve to transmit flight or landing loads — such as frames to which the wing, control surfaces, or similar components are attached — must naturally not be made from lime.

For control surface horns and for the skid, which during landing is subjected to considerable bending and also abrasion, we use the tough and resilient ash.

For filling corners and other fairing work (the fairing tail of the cockpit enclosure, fairings over protruding fittings, etc.), balsa wood is used. This is distinguished by its extraordinarily low weight and ease of workability.

When a piece of balsa wood has been shaped and is to be smoothed, the surface is brushed several times with cold glue mixed to a thin consistency. This creates a hard surface that takes varnish well. Without this treatment, the soft fibres make it impossible to achieve a smooth finish.

---

GLUE.

In aircraft construction only cold glue, also called casein glue, is used. The glue is supplied as a flour-like powder and is mixed with water ready for use. Nothing may be glued with joiner's glue or hot glue, as this is neither water-resistant nor possesses the necessary bonding strength.

PLYWOOD.

For joining strips, and for skinning wing leading edges, fuselages, etc., plywood is used. Plywood is normally glued together from three thin layers, of which the two outer layers run lengthwise and the inner layer runs crosswise. In glider construction we work with plywood from 0.8 to 3 mm, or occasionally 4 mm thickness; the latter is rarely used, as it is only required for torsion noses of very large cantilever aircraft.

The skinning of highly stressed parts, such as spars, wing leading edges, fuselages, and connecting fishplates of diagonals, etc., may only be carried out using birch plywood of aircraft quality. Parts that serve purely as fairing, as well as corner pieces in ribs or similar applications, may be worked with lower grades. However, these must be carefully selected, and defective pieces must be rejected before use. Defective areas are immediately recognisable by holding the panel up against the sun or against a strong electric light bulb. Voids in the middle layer then appear as bright spots. For the outer layers the same applies as for normal timber: no knots, cracks, etc. When dealing with the manufacture of individual components, the possibility of using lower grades will be addressed again.

STEEL FOR CONICAL BOLTS FOR WING ATTACHMENT FITTINGS

Conical bolts for wing attachment fittings are normally turned from SM steel. For bolts subject to particularly high loads, the steel grades marketed under the following designations are suitable: weldable steel St.C.25.61, non-weldable steel St.C.45.61.

LIGHT METALS: ELEKTRON AND DURAL

For cable pulleys and parts of segment levers over which cables run, Elektron pressed material or Galalith is used. No other materials should be used for this purpose, as cables running only on these materials suffer little wear.

**Dural Sheet**

Dural sheet is used only in rare cases in glider construction, since the working of Dural requires special equipment for heat treatment after bending operations. Clubs cannot normally afford this, and club workshop operations can be managed perfectly well without Dural sheet. Should the need to fabricate Dural fittings arise, however, one should send for the Dural processing literature, from which everything worth knowing about its working can be found.

**Dural Tube**

For elevator bearing tubes and controls that require only drilling and little other machining, grade 681a, hardness 1, from Dürener Metallwerke is very suitable, with an ultimate tensile strength of 40 kg/mm² and an elongation of approximately 10%.

## WIRES AND CABLES

**Wire**
For bracing lattice tails, cross-bracing of struts, or similar purposes, steel wire is used. The use of iron wire for bracing is not permitted in aircraft construction.

**Control cables**
For control runs, use only cable with a hemp core (trade designation DIN L 9). Control cables are wound from individual strands laid around a hemp core. Each strand is itself twisted from many fine wires, the individual diameter of which must not exceed 0.3 mm, as otherwise the cable becomes too stiff and runs over the pulley with excessive friction loss.

Do not use old cables with individual broken wire strands, as such fracture points rapidly enlarge

---

*=== PAGE 21 ===*

and can easily lead to a complete break. Bowden cables, or cables without a hemp core, are not permitted.

## COVERING

The covering fabric for gliders and sailplanes must be selected according to the intended use of the aircraft. For training sailplanes, a firm cretonne or linen is sufficient, while for high-performance machines a lighter material such as silk batiste or raw silk is preferred. Should there be any possibility that the latter machines will be used for cloud flying, where the covering is at risk from hail damage, then covering with the firm yet light raw silk — or alternatively with the admittedly heavier cretonne or linen — is absolutely essential. Silk batiste becomes brittle after doping with Cellon and cannot withstand hail impact.

We can see the effect hail damage can have from the elevators of the *Fafnir*. The well-known sailplane pilot Gronhoff encountered severe hail squalls during one of his cross-country flights, which caused considerable damage to the wing and elevator covering (Fig. 10). The covering was made of silk batiste.

*Fig. 10. Elevator covering destroyed by hail damage.*

When selecting covering fabric, we always first purchase a sample piece and test its suitability with regard to doping with Cellon and lacquering. On a frame (see later under "Doping with Cellon") we stretch the piece, apply the Cellon dope, and determine whether the fabric tightens correctly, does not absorb too much Cellon, is airtight, and does not become too brittle. Coarse-weave fabrics in particular consume a large amount of Cellon.

FABRIC STRENGTH

A minimum strength of 6 kg/cm in warp and weft is required of the fabric. Almost any fine-weave linen or cotton fabric is suitable for training aircraft, provided it is only lightly sized.

---

IMPREGNATION AGENTS FOR COVERING FABRIC

For covering beginner gliders, the fabric may be impregnated with waterglass or starch. These agents tauten the fabric and make it reasonably airtight. Such an aircraft should not be left standing in the rain for long, as the whole covering will otherwise soften.

**Waterglass**
For impregnation with waterglass, only cretonne with sizing is suitable. Waterglass is available at any chemist's shop. One coat is sufficient. The waterglass may need to be thinned somewhat with water.

**Starch**
Any fabric can be impregnated with rice starch, prepared in the same way as shirt starch. The starch should however be applied warm.

**Cellon**
The covering of all other sailplanes must be treated with Cellon. Cellon is likewise manufactured by numerous firms; not every Cellon is compatible with every type of fabric. Again, order samples first and check suitability. After two to three coats the fabric must be properly taut and airtight. (Test by drawing air through with the mouth.)

---

WOOD IMPREGNATION

Internal aircraft components are best protected with a colourless spirit varnish. This is inexpensive, dries quickly, and is easily removed during repairs, as it contains no oil and therefore does not penetrate into the wood. The Cellon varnish "Ocker," manufactured by Titanin-Werke, has also proven very satisfactory. In addition to fast drying, a coat of this varnish provides the further advantage that the yellow finish remains clearly visible, ensuring no unprotected spots are left, and the interior of the aircraft always appears clean.

FINISHING WITH VARNISH

Those who must work economically may also paint the exterior of the aircraft with spirit varnish; however, its durability is low and a high degree of smoothness cannot be achieved. The best high-gloss finish is obtained with a good boat varnish. Using inferior varnishes is pointless, as the price evens out through greater durability.

The wood is first impregnated with a pore filler, then sanded and coated with the finish varnish.

TREATMENT OF FITTINGS

All fittings are protected against rust with a clear finish varnish (blue or green). The varnish must be transparent in order to allow inspection of the fittings. As already mentioned above, the coloured varnishes have the advantage of being easy to see.

MATERIAL PROCESSING

GLUING

**Mixing the glue**

As already mentioned, only cold casein glue is used in aircraft construction. The cold glue powder is mixed with water. Each manufacturer supplies appropriate instructions for use, which must be followed exactly. The cold glue is mixed in enamel, stoneware, or glass vessels — metal containers will spoil the glue — using a wooden spatula, and only in quantities that will be used within the next eight hours, as the glue has a limited working life.

**Working life**

(Barely 10 hours; in high temperatures the working life is reduced to 6 hours.) Never use leftover glue from the previous day.

Glue powder must be stored dry and in airtight containers, as it deteriorates on exposure to air.

Glue only at normal temperature. Frost destroys any glued joint.

**Clamping**

For a durable glued joint, adequate clamping pressure is the most important factor. Do not apply the glue too thinly; it must squeeze out from the joints. Do not wash off the squeezed-out glue — it may only be removed with a spatula after one hour.

---

*[Page 24]*

The clamping device must remain in place for 6 hours, and further processing should not begin until after 12 hours. In summer, during very dry weather, these times are reduced considerably. The glue that has been squeezed out can serve as a gauge of correct bonding — it must be almost hard.

Depending on the location of the glued joint, the clamping devices also vary. The most important aircraft construction equipment — clamps — has already been mentioned. To transfer the pressure of the individual clamps across the entire gluing surface during pressing, packing pieces are used, i.e. thick wooden strips placed between the workpiece and the clamps. In addition, the wood grain is protected from damage by the clamps by means of these packing pieces.

**Clamps**

Another type of clamp was shown in Figure 3. This can be used in places where screw clamps cannot easily reach. The steel clamps are spread apart using suitable pliers and then applied.

All pieces to be glued must fit together cleanly, whether fitted into or onto one another. No voids must be allowed to form which are simply filled with glue, with the assumption: "Now everything is in order — nothing can be seen anymore."

# Nailing Strips

When applying plywood (fuselage walls, nose planking, or corner joints), nailing strips are used. For this purpose, strips of 10 × 5 or 15 × 5 mm are taken, and nails are driven through the strip into the glue joint (Fig. 11). Since the nailing strips distribute the pressure evenly, it is not necessary to drive nails right next to one another. When the glue has dried, the nailing strip together with the nails is pulled out using pliers.

# Nail Strips for Curved Parts

For pressing round parts or pressing against them, plywood strips (d = 2 mm) are used instead of solid strips. (Applying plywood to wing leading edges.)

Nailing strips should always be prepared in generous quantities, since they are in constant use. Nails of 15 to 18 mm length are nailed into the strips at intervals of 15 to 20 mm so that only the tips of the nails protrude; later the strips need only be placed on the glue joint and the nails driven through firmly.

# Use of Nails

In a good glue joint, nails do not increase the strength of the connection. The fewer nails that remain in the wood, the better for the service life and appearance of the aircraft interior. Iron nails must be removed in all cases, as they rust and destroy the wood fibres. No nails whatsoever in wing spar flanges!

# Wood Screws

Never use nails that are too large, or wood screws, for pressing. Spar flanges, struts, and diagonals in particular must be protected from damage by large nails — every nail destroys wood fibres.

# Roughening Surfaces

For highly stressed glue joints, such as the spar, segment, and control surface planking, the wood onto which the plywood is to be glued must be roughened. With hardwood in particular, this roughening is always necessary, as it bonds less well than softwoods. For roughening, a toothed plane is required. It is set fine, and the gluing surface is first roughened diagonally across the grain and then perpendicular to it. The plane iron must be adjusted so that it cuts only very fine grooves into the wood.

LACQUERING

To achieve an absolutely flawless smooth surface, the following must be observed during lacquering:

Spray the workshop floor with water and sweep it out.
If possible, do not fire the stove any further; if the temperature cannot be maintained, heat with great caution.
Keep windows and doors closed to avoid draughts.
Refrain from all other work and unnecessary movement about the workshop.

All of this serves only to prevent dust from being stirred up, as it is the worst enemy when applying a smooth surface and allowing the lacquer to dry.

**Top Coat**

The temperature should be approximately 15°C. Using a normal, not too small, hair brush, apply the lacquer evenly over the wing, always stroking in the direction of flight. Apply as thinly as possible, do not work over any one spot for too long, and take care not to miss any uncoated areas. On steeply inclined surfaces in particular, do not apply the lacquer too thickly, as it will otherwise begin to run after a short time, forming unsightly drips and ripples. Reference should again be made to the test frame. If this is tilted during test lacquering, one will quickly notice how thickly the lacquer may be applied before it begins to run.

Boat lacquer is dust-dry after approximately 12 hours, but may require up to 36 hours to dry completely. Then wash all lacquered surfaces with cold water and a chamois leather. This may be repeated several times; it is this process that gives the lacquer its proper hardness.

# CONTROL LEVERS

The ailerons, elevator, and rudder are operated by levers. The manufacture of these levers must be carried out with great care, as the failure of one would have catastrophic consequences.

Levers with thicknesses of 12 to 18 mm are made from ash or pine and faced on both sides with 2 mm plywood. The wood grain must run perpendicular to the direction of stress (Fig. 60). The outer grain of the plywood facing runs in the same direction. In many cases it is also glued at right angles to this. The former method, however, appears to be the better one and is most commonly used. To achieve a good glue joint, the gluing surfaces must be roughened.

Into the ends of the levers, where the control cable or the relevant fitting is attached, a copper tube rivet must be inserted. Copper tube 8×1 mm or 6×1 mm is inserted into the pre-drilled hole and riveted over using the drift shown in Figure 61, which can be made by the worker himself. Before riveting over, the tube projects approximately 3 to 4 mm above and below the surface.

# WINGTIP BOWS

Curved strips are used in all manner of locations, most commonly as form-giving members — for example on elevators and rudders, fuselage noses, wing tips, and so forth. In some cases the required curvature is too sharp for the cross-section involved, so that it cannot be bent from a single strip. Such a bow is therefore made from several thin strips — also called laminations — which are bent and glued in a block or nail former. Figure 62 shows the pressing of the strips with screw clamps in a nail former of the type that must be used for load-bearing bows.

A different method is shown in Figure 63. First, nails are driven along the inner circumference of the bow, then the individual strips, coated with the specified glue, are pressed together by nails driven as closely as possible to the outside of the strips all the way around. In addition, the nails are drawn even more tightly against the strips using binding wire.

It may also occur that the strips must be bent not merely in a single plane but must follow a three-dimensional curve (fuselage nose or similar). For this purpose, the bow is first drawn on a flat board in plan view, and blocks are placed at certain intervals; these blocks must be as high as the amount by which the strip rises out of the plane. If the out-of-plane bend is very pronounced, the bow is first glued up in one plane, then cut back into individual laminations and drawn around in the other direction (Fig. 64).

# TRAILING EDGE STRIPS

Trailing edge strips form the finishing edge of wing or tail surface trailing edges, around which the fabric is glued. The fabric, pulled taut by the doping process, will bend poorly designed trailing edge strips out of true. This is effectively prevented by inserting a plywood fin (Fig. 65). The standard cross-section of trailing edge strips is 10 × 10 mm. The plywood is slid into a groove milled 5 mm deep using the specified glue (Fig. 66a), or two strips of 5 × 10 mm are glued to each side of the fin (Fig. 66b).

Anyone who prefers a pointed trailing edge strip may construct it along the lines of Figure 66c. The trailing edge strips described above have proven themselves best in practice and are well established. The belief that pointed trailing edge strips improve aircraft performance is mistaken. So build trailing edge strips with a square cross-section without hesitation.

# INSTALLING FITTINGS

Fittings required for assembling the individual aircraft components serve the purpose of transferring loads from wooden parts through the fittings back into wooden parts. The greatest care must therefore be devoted to achieving a sound connection between the fittings and the wood.

The standard fastening element is the hexagon-head bolt. The use of tubular rivets will rarely be applicable in club workshop operations.

Since the fitting transfers loads partly through friction — generated by the clamping pressure against the wood produced by the bolts — and partly through

---

*[Continues page 77]*

**Fitting and Drilling Guidelines for Hardware Attachments**

...held in the wood by the bolt bearing against the hole walls, attention must be paid to these two factors.

Good adhesion of the fitting to the wood is achieved by tightening the screws evenly and correctly. Do not over-tighten, as this compresses the wood and deforms the fitting; there is also the risk of stripping the thread. In time, one develops the proper feel for tightening a screw.

---

**Gluing Fittings**

To further increase friction between the fitting and the wood, a mixture of cobbler's pitch and beeswax in a 1:1 ratio can be prepared. This compound is applied warm to the wood, the fitting is positioned and tightened down. A blowtorch is then used to heat the fitting from the outside, and the screws are tightened further until the adhesive compound appears at the edges of the fitting. Fittings attached in this manner can afterwards only be removed from the wood with great difficulty. Gluing fittings in place is naturally only justified for highly stressed fittings, for example wing attachment fittings on high-performance aircraft.

---

**Burning Holes**

Furthermore, the holes drilled in the wood are of importance for a sound fitting installation. Never burn holes for safety-critical fittings. The surrounding wood fibres are destroyed during hole-burning, and the hole wall yields under subsequent loading by the bolt, resulting in loose-sitting screws.

Drill perpendicularly — do not work the drill back and forth until you happen to come out at the right spot. If one lacks practice, a colleague can assist by positioning himself three to four metres away and sighting first from the front and then from the side to check whether the drill is being applied perpendicularly to the workpiece. It is always advisable, when working with heavy structural members, to pre-drill first with a thin drill bit, and then to ream and bore out to the correct final diameter.

---

If we notice that a hole has gone astray, we must not be so irresponsible as to drive a screw in at such a point and simply leave it at that. Such a screw holds virtually nothing. If the packing block is too small, or is positioned in the wrong...

## APPLYING PLYWOOD LEADING EDGE SKINS

Applying plywood over large leading edges requires practice and has caused many a glider builder considerable trouble.

First, let us be clear about what a properly applied plywood leading edge must look like — one that also meets the structural strength requirements placed upon it.

The grain of the plywood runs in most cases along the length of the leading edge. The applied plywood must be well glued to the ribs and spars. It should not be applied so wet that the ribs are pressed through. The plywood must be undamaged — that is, it must have no cuts from careless work with sharp tools, and no cracks from bending the plywood too sharply. The individual panel joints must be executed cleanly, with no slipped or frayed scarf joints. In standard construction, the plywood finishes flush with the trailing edge of the spar. Care must be taken that it bears against the full width of the spar. Finally, the leading edge skin must not be applied in such a way that the wing warps. ## APPLYING A NOSE SKIN WITH NAIL STRIPS

The wing is clamped correctly onto the building jig with clamps. If a jig trestle happens to be positioned in the area of the nose section to be skinned, it must be removed from in front of the spar and replaced with another support that provides adequate support to the spar. It is pressed firmly using nail strips — two or three placed side by side for very wide spars.

Always begin nailing from the centre of the panel outward, since wrinkles easily form if you work from the ends toward the centre. At the upper edge of the plywood we attach several hold-down clamps or screw clamps (do not forget the packing pieces) and use these to draw the plywood around the nose. Under continuous firm tension, the skin is nailed down with the help of nail strips. Take care that the nails are also driven into the ribs so that the correct pressure is achieved. ## Rigidity

**Ailerons** are in most cases assembled completely on the wing and then cut free. If it turns out that they are too flexible, they must be stiffened.

---

*[Page 95 of original]*

If the diagonals are already installed, one must observe when twisting the control surface whether the diagonals or ribs deflect in any way. This must then be prevented by gluing on plywood gussets and inserting webs. The best way to achieve sufficient rigidity is by fully planking the ribs and diagonals with plywood.

The question now arises as to what should be considered soft in a control surface. Based on tests carried out on standard training aircraft control surfaces, the following rule of thumb can be established:

When checking a control surface for rigidity, grasp it at approximately 1.2 m from the segment lever rib at the trailing edge and twist it by pushing down with one hand and up with the other. The experienced aircraft builder can assess the rigidity of the control surface on the basis of the twist. The inexperienced builder can form a judgement by supporting the control surface trailing edge at the segment lever rib and loading it at 1.2 m distance with 10 kg. On a normally rigid control surface, this point deflects by approximately 4–5 cm. This measurement is of course approximate, but can serve as an initial guide when assessing control surface rigidity.

# RAW ASSEMBLY (ROHBAUMONTAGE)

In the raw assembly stage, the aircraft is completely assembled, with bracing wires and cables installed. As mentioned earlier, raw assembly is always advisable, since any mistakes that may occur can still be corrected easily at this stage.

**What must be observed during assembly?**

At connection fittings where bolts will not enter freely, do not use force. Much can often be achieved by reaming with a reamer; filing must be avoided under all circumstances, as the holes will become oval and the connection fitting will become loose after only a short time in service.

It is practical to keep all connection bores 1 millimetre undersize, and only to ream them to the correct diameter at the time of assembly.

*Fig. 146. Zögling with elastic suspension for the upper bracing wires.*

A drift pin is used to hold the bore in the desired position. The tip of the connection bolt is filed to a taper to ease insertion. The bolt must be drivable with light hammer blows. Do not strike with a 2 kg hammer as though our machine were an anvil.

On braced aircraft — for example the Zögling — the upper wires are installed first, with the turnbuckles wound half open. The rubber-cord suspension for the upper wires has proven very effective (Fig. 146). During heavy "bounce" landings

## ASSEMBLY OF BRACED AND CANTILEVER AIRCRAFT

In braced aircraft, the wings are hung on the fuselage and then supported and aligned by means of battens or trestles. The struts are assembled at the fuselage first. The fittings are held in position on the strut by means of clamps. The struts are then fitted and the fittings drilled through. By fastening the fittings afterwards, we relieve ourselves of a major concern: our aircraft will have the correct angle of incidence, provided that we have sighted correctly when supporting the wing.

For wing surfaces on high-performance aircraft, the wing attachments are often made by means of tapered bolts. The wing is assembled in the normal manner, and through the holes that are to be reamed out for the tapered bolts, suitable bolts are inserted to hold the wing panel in position. The panel is then firmly supported, one attachment bolt is withdrawn, and the hole is reamed out with a tapered reamer matching the bolt.

A round file will not produce a proper fit. If it is difficult to work with the reamer in the narrow wing gap, weld a steel tube extension onto the reamer shank.

If, when sighting along the fully assembled wings, it becomes apparent that they have different angles of incidence on the two sides, we must determine exactly where the fault lies. If the wing is twisted throughout, the attachment fittings have been set on inaccurately. This is, however, rarely the case; far more frequently the wing warps during the application of the wet plywood leading-edge skin. At the point where one wing begins to be warped, the leading-edge skin must be stripped off and re-applied. In some circumstances the wing is clamped on the building jig in the direction opposite to the warp and re-skinned. When removed from the jig, the error is then exactly compensated. Judging the correct amount of counter-twist requires a practical feel that can only be acquired through extended experience.

# GLIDER

*Instructions for Self-Building by H. Jacobs. Volume 138 of the Series "Play and Work" ..... Price RM. 3.50*

The aircraft whose construction is treated exemplarily through all stages of building in this work is a braced high-wing monoplane with a boat-hull fuselage and a 12 m wingspan. It is suited both to training flights and to multi-hour soaring flights. Designed in its basic form by A. Lippisch, it was built in series by Schleicher and has now been substantially improved once more for the purposes of self-building. The construction of the aircraft is straightforward and inexpensive relative to its excellent soaring characteristics.

The 78-page building description is made comprehensible to everyone by the 86 photographs and drawings alone; the 4 large construction plans in particular provide an entirely reliable foundation for faultless execution of the build and successful results.

# SPORT FLYING

*An Introduction to Technique and Practice. Edited by Dr.-Ing. Werner von Langsdorff with the collaboration of distinguished pilots, engineers and scientists of German aviation. With numerous photographs and drawings. A publication of the year 1935. Price approx. RM. 3.50*

This book aims to outline the entire field of sport flying for the sport pilot and for those who wish to become one. To that end, the physical and technical fundamentals of flight, the practical handling of sporting aircraft, and the meaning of sport flying are treated in a manner accessible to all.

In order to keep the presentations true to life, all sections were written exclusively by men with many years of their own practical flying experience. Following a foreword by Air Commodore Friedrich Christiansen, the contributions of flight leaders Loerzer, Geyer, Ritter v. Schleich, Stamer, Carganico, Achgelis, Hirth, and others are brought together under the editorship of the editor himself — a noted designer and record-breaking pilot — into an altogether excellent work, one destined to carry forward the development of German air sport, which is built upon the broad foundation of gliding, into the wider field of powered flight.
